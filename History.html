History

'AARON' the AI Art Generator

AARON, Harold Cohen's revolutionary artificial intelligence (AI) program, produced the first piece of AI art. This artwork from 1972 marks a significant turning point in the creativity computers could produce. Its algorithmic approach to art creation sparked conversations about AI's role in artistic expression and the nature of creativity itself. Engineers and artists alike continue to be inspired by AARON's legacy, pushing ongoing study into the crossroads of AI and the arts, shaping the evolving nature of digital creativity.

From 1972 to 2010, Harold Cohen developed AARON further. The application was not open-source, and thus, once Cohen passed away in 2016, development on it was put on hold. In the 1980s, after beginning with abstract paintings that grew increasingly detailed over time, AARON began "drawing" rocks, plants, and even humans. Colour was introduced in the 1990s, and in the 2000s, AARON resumed creating abstract art, but this time with colour.

Cohen created AARON's tangible artwork using machinery. Originally, black-and-white drawings were made using specially designed flatbed plotter gear. Occasionally, Cohen would hand-color these pictures or enlarge them for larger paintings. He created computerized painting devices for the production of fabric dye and ink in the 1990s. Afterwards, AARON's photos were printed using a large-scale inkjet printer onto canvases.

Genetic Images

Karl Sims is a computer graphic artist who used genetic algorithms to create digital art. In "Genetic Images," one of his most well-known efforts, he employed genetic algorithms to create abstract visuals. Users would choose from a grid of alternatives the photographs they thought looked good, creating a new generation of photos by combining and altering the selected images. This approach showcased how evolutionary algorithms could be used creatively.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

In 2014, Ian Goodfellow and associates created a class of machine learning frameworks called Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). A GAN is made up of two neural networks: a discriminator that assesses the validity of generated data and a generator that creates data similar to the training set. The two networks are trained simultaneously, with the generator aiming to trick the discriminator. This adversarial process forces the generator to produce more realistic outputs. GANs have become popular for applications like data augmentation and image production, advancing AI art, virtual reality, and deepfake technology.

Stable Diffusion

Diffusion models generate complex data, like images, by transforming random noise through a continuous process. Unlike GANs, they are more stable, easier to control, and can produce high-quality images with lower computational costs, while avoiding issues like mode collapse (when the AI art generator fails to produce diverse outputs). These advantages have made diffusion models a popular alternative to GANs in AI art. The models have made AI art more accessible, fueling the growth of web-based AI art generators.
